Attention-Based Neural Quantum States

A transformer neural network that solves the quantum many-body problem: variational, GPU-accelerated, and built stage by stage against exact references. CodeWarm-start solver

Left: a skyrmion crystal from the trained network. Right: four candidate states converging to their energy plateaus (18 electrons).

A transformer wavefunction (left) trained by variational Monte Carlo with MinSR natural-gradient updates (right).

The quantum many-body problem, finding the ground state of many interacting electrons, is exponentially hard. Neural quantum states (NQS) attack it by representing the wavefunction as a neural network and optimizing it with variational Monte Carlo. The network is self-supervised, the data is self-generated, and the loss function is the variational energy.

I built a continuum NQS solver in JAX. The architecture is inspired by the electron-token self-attention design of DeepMind's Psiformer. I extended it to a two-dimensional electron gas with spin–orbit coupling, a Zeeman field, and long-range Coulomb interaction. Unlike the molecular Psiformer, the wavefunction here is periodic, complex-valued, and spinful. The network is optimized by variational Monte Carlo with natural-gradient (MinSR) updates, running on UW's Tillicum H200 GPU cluster and Google Cloud Platform.

To an ML engineer, the training loop is familiar territory. The energy gradient is a score-function (REINFORCE-style) estimator with a variance-reducing baseline, and the natural-gradient (Fisher) update is solved in sample space through a kernel-trick identity (MinSR), which is what makes second-order optimization affordable for the full network. Robustness comes from the standard toolkit: zero-initialization so the network starts exactly at a validated reference state, learning-rate annealing, outlier-robust (median-MAD) clipping, early stopping with dedicated unbiased evaluation passes, and multi-seed restarts.

Before trusting the network with physics, I benchmarked the architecture against exact diagonalization of the same Hamiltonian. The trained network lands 0.7 mHa above the exact ground state at two electrons, within Monte-Carlo noise, recovering 96% of the correlation energy. Every design choice is grounded in a published precedent or a theorem, and a size-scaling ablation selected the final ansatz. The convergence panel above shows a large-scale run at 18 electrons: several competing candidate states, each optimized to a tight energy plateau and compared.

The warm start: a from-scratch Hartree–Fock engine

The network does not start from random weights. Its spinor envelope is warm-started from Hartree–Fock: the training begins at a validated mean-field state, and the network's job is exactly the correlation energy that Hartree–Fock leaves out. Those starting states come from a second from-scratch solver: a Python self-consistent-field Hartree–Fock engine for the same family of systems (a triangular-lattice Hubbard model with Rashba spin–orbit coupling, and the continuum electron gas the NQS targets).

The warm-start engine at a glance. Left: the self-consistent loop iterates a symmetry-breaking trial order to a fixed point of the free energy. Top right: a production run converges five orders of magnitude in the residual. Bottom right: an in-plane seed grows out-of-plane skyrmion cores; converged textures like this one become the network's starting points.

The engine is a competition, not a single run: each candidate phase is seeded from a library of ~35 magnetic textures, iterated with linear and Pulay (DIIS) mixing, and the winner is chosen by energy ordering rather than by the seed. Every converged state also gets a topology verdict (winding number, Chern number by two independent methods that must agree, Hall conductivity). Mean-field campaigns run at scale on NERSC's Perlmutter and Azure (hundreds of self-consistent solutions per parameter scan), and the ~7k-line, 77-test solver core is open source; see the full solver write-up for the numerical details.

The trained network finds a skyrmion crystal as a locally stable state, shown in the first figure above. Warm-started from a Hartree–Fock mean-field seed, the texture does not melt, and its winding survives correlations intact. Although nearly degenerate with a uniform quantum fluid, the skyrmion crystal remains a competitive, well-defined phase.
