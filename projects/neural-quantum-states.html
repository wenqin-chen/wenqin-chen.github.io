Attention-Based Neural Quantum States

A transformer neural network that solves the quantum many-body problem: variational, GPU-accelerated, and built stage by stage against exact references.

  • JAX
  • Transformers / attention
  • Variational Monte Carlo
  • Natural-gradient optimization (SR / MinSR)
  • GPU (H200) & SLURM / HPC
  • Reproducible research code
  • pytest

Left: a spin texture from the trained network: a skyrmion crystal, with out-of-plane spin in colour and in-plane spin as arrows winding around each core (the green rhombus is one magnetic cell). Right: the 18-electron training run: four candidate states, each optimized by natural-gradient (MinSR) variational Monte Carlo, converging to their energy plateaus.

In plain terms: a transformer defines the wavefunction, and the training loop tunes it to minimize the energy. The full pipeline: a transformer wavefunction (left) trained by variational Monte Carlo with MinSR natural-gradient updates (right). Learnable components in red, fixed physics in grey; the production ansatz sums four generalized determinants and carries a flexible Jastrow prefactor (not drawn).

The quantum many-body problem, finding the ground state of many interacting electrons, is exponentially hard. Neural quantum states (NQS) attack it by representing the wavefunction as a neural network and optimizing it with variational Monte Carlo.

I built a continuum NQS solver in JAX. The architecture is inspired by the electron-token self-attention design of DeepMind's Psiformer. I extended it to a two-dimensional electron gas with spin–orbit coupling, a Zeeman field, and long-range Coulomb interaction. Unlike the molecular Psiformer, the wavefunction here is periodic, complex-valued, and spinful. The network is optimized by variational Monte Carlo with natural-gradient (MinSR) updates, running on UW's Tillicum H200 GPU cluster and Google Cloud Platform.

Before trusting the network with physics, I put the architecture through verification against exact diagonalization of the same Hamiltonian. The trained network lands 0.7 mHa above the exact ground state at two electrons, within Monte-Carlo noise (96% of the correlation energy). Every design choice is grounded in a published precedent or a theorem, and a size-scaling ablation selected the production ansatz. The convergence panel above shows a production run at 18 electrons: several competing candidate states, each optimized to a tight energy plateau and compared.

The trained network finds a skyrmion crystal as a locally stable state, shown in the figure above. Optimized from a Hartree–Fock mean-field seed, the texture does not melt, and its winding survives correlations intact. Although nearly degenerate with a uniform quantum fluid, the skyrmion crystal remains a competitive, well-defined phase.
