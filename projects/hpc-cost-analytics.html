Pricing HPC Runs Before Launch

Every solver run on a cluster costs real wall-clock time, and nearly half of my flagship compute was going to runs that never converged. So I treated my own research computing as a data problem: two years of telemetry from three solver codebases, assembled into a queryable warehouse and turned into a cost model and a calibrated scheduling policy. The pipeline, the data, and every analysis decision are public.

The warehouse and the model. Left: 2,127 runs spanning four orders of magnitude in cost. Right: out-of-campaign predictions from the gradient-boosted cost model against the actual cost; the diagonal is a perfect prediction.

The dataset is itself an engineering deliverable. The research behind the telemetry is unpublished, so every export passes through a redaction contract: column whitelists, salted opaque ids for physical parameters, anonymized campaign codes, and a leak-audit pytest suite that scans every string value against forbidden patterns and runs in CI on every push. What leaves the private repos is solver telemetry only: iterations, residuals, wall-clock, grid sizes, seed families, machines.

The data-quality pass drives everything downstream, and its decisions are recorded inline in the notebooks. Runs that hit their iteration budget are censored (their recorded cost is only a lower bound); in one source that is a third of the runs holding two thirds of the wall-hours, so they are kept and flagged rather than dropped. Multi-seed jobs share one timer, so their per-run costs are apportioned and stress-tested. And the two tables that look joinable share exactly one campaign code out of 34 and 78, so the analysis compares distributions instead of forcing a fuzzy join.

The model is evaluated the honest way. Runs within a campaign are near-duplicates, and a random cross-validation split lets the model score itself on copies of its training rows: the same gradient-boosted model reads R² = 0.82 under a random split but 0.21 under leave-campaign-out. That gap is leakage, measured. Reported honestly, the model is typically 2.8x off pooled (2.5x on the dominant source), with fold-level spread from 1.4x on large homogeneous campaigns to nearly useless on one-off campaigns. Feature importance is measured on held-out campaigns too, which shows the operator-set knobs (iteration budget, tolerance) acting as proxies for expected difficulty, while the k-grid density is the physical driver: cost scales like its 6.4th power.

The payoff is a scheduling policy with a guarantee. Split conformal prediction turns point predictions into walltime requests that hit a chosen safety level: 95% empirical coverage at 4.6x the prediction, replacing the measured 17x blanket cushion in my actual scheduler requests, with the cold-start regime honestly scoped out (a genuinely new campaign needs roughly 100x). A planned part 3 replays this policy and a cheap-first seed ordering against the recorded launch history.
