Braiding Majorana Zero Modes

Two theory papers — one first-author — on encoding and braiding Majorana zero modes in topological-superconductor nanowires: the building blocks of a fault-tolerant topological qubit.

  • Role — First author (PRB) · co-author (SCPMA)
  • Venue — Phys. Rev. B 105 (2022) · Sci. China Phys. Mech. Astron. 64 (2021)
  • Method — Bogoliubov–de Gennes theory

A topological superconductor — a semiconductor nanowire with strong spin–orbit coupling, proximity-induced superconductivity, and a magnetic (Zeeman) field — binds a Majorana zero mode at each end (γL, γR). Each is "half" of an electron; together they hold one qubit in their shared fermion parity, so no local disturbance at either end can read or corrupt it.

Quantum computers are fragile: local noise corrupts the delicate states they compute with, and most architectures fight this with heavy layers of error correction. Topological quantum computing takes a different route — it stores information non-locally, so protection is built into the physics rather than patched on afterward.

The building block is the Majorana zero mode (MZM) — an emergent, zero-energy excitation that appears in pairs at the ends of a topological superconductor (diagram above). Each is "half" of an ordinary electron; a widely separated pair shares one fermion state, so a single qubit lives in the joint parity of two modes at opposite ends of a wire. No local measurement on either end alone can tell which state the qubit is in — that is where the noise-protection comes from.

What makes them compute is how you manipulate them. Exchanging — braiding — two Majoranas applies a quantum gate that depends only on the topology of the exchange, not on how fast or how precisely you do it. Crucially the exchanges do not commute: they are non-Abelian, so braids generate genuine, intrinsically robust quantum gates.

Braiding as worldlines: time runs upward, position runs across. Exchanging Majoranas γ2 and γ3 (one strand passing over the other) applies a quantum gate that depends only on which modes are swapped and in what order — not on timing or path. Different orders give different results (the gates don't commute — non-Abelian), which is exactly what lets braids compute.

The hard part is physics, not wiring. To build a real topological qubit you have to (i) engineer a genuine topological superconductor, and (ii) braid its Majoranas robustly — all while telling a true Majorana apart from mundane look-alikes such as trivial Andreev bound states, which produce nearly the same experimental signatures. I worked on both problems in two theory papers, sketched below.

Left (Paper 1): a modified tetron — four gate-controlled nanowires — with a trivial Andreev bound state present; a geometric-phase cancellation keeps the braiding statistics robust. Right (Paper 2): the smallest device that can braid — two wires coupled through one quantum dot, braided by looping the couplings (a Berry phase) and read out as an electric current.

The two papers

Non-Abelian statistics in the presence of an Andreev bound state (first author, Phys. Rev. B 105, 054507). I studied braiding in a modified tetron — four coupled superconductor–semiconductor nanowires with gate control — but with a realistic contaminant present: a trivial Andreev bound state, the most common experimental mimic of a Majorana, modeled as a pair of weakly coupled Majoranas. The ABS hybridizes with the braided modes and injects a spurious, braiding-time-dependent phase that would spoil the statistics. I showed that a geometric-phase (spin-echo-like) cancellation removes that dynamical phase and recovers braiding-time-independent non-Abelian statistics even with the contaminant present — and that the same gate-tunable coupling can be turned into controllable single-qubit rotations.

A minimal setup for non-Abelian braiding (co-developed, second author, Sci. China Phys. Mech. Astron. 64, 117811). This proposes the smallest device that can braid: two topological-superconductor nanowires coupled through a single quantum dot. Instead of physically dragging Majoranas around a two-dimensional network, braiding is done by adiabatically tracing a closed loop in the dot's coupling-parameter space — a geometric (Berry-phase) braid. The operation flips the local fermion parity, which converts into a measurable electric current: an electrical read-out of non-Abelian statistics.

This was my earliest research area — a first, hands-on engagement (one first-author paper, one co-authored) with the core problems of topological quantum computing: realizing Majorana modes, braiding them robustly, and separating true Majoranas from trivial look-alikes.
